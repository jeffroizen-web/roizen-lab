Vitamin D is one of the most recommended therapies in medicine — and one of the least understood.

We are changing that.

We aim to understand non-calcium effects of vitamin D — and to exploit this understanding into new therapies for common, poorly managed diseases like sarcopenia and polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS).

Research

Big Questions

Each project in our lab is anchored by a question about vitamin D that challenges conventional thinking.

01

How does vitamin D redirect calories from fat to muscle?

We discovered that high-dose vitamin D reallocates surplus calories to muscle and linear growth instead of fat, through modulation of myostatin and leptin signaling. This finding connects a ubiquitous supplement to the fundamental biology of body composition.

02

Why do obese individuals have lower vitamin D levels?

Our work revealed that obesity decreases hepatic 25-hydroxylase activity, providing a mechanistic explanation for this well-documented clinical association. The relationship between vitamin D and obesity is bidirectional — and more complex than anyone expected.

03

Can vitamin D modulate tumor biology?

We are investigating how dietary vitamin D influences tumor engraftment through regulation of vitamin D binding protein (GC protein) abundance. This unexpected finding opens a new dimension in understanding vitamin D's role beyond bone and calcium.

04

Does vitamin D status during development shape lifelong health?

We study how vitamin D levels in children affect growth, immune development, and susceptibility to autoimmune conditions like type 1 diabetes. As a pediatric endocrinologist, Jeff sees these patients every day — the bench-to-bedside arc is direct.

Phenotype

Vitamin D and Body Composition

High-dose dietary vitamin D reallocates surplus calories from fat to muscle and linear growth. Using CYP2R1 knockout mice — animals that lack the enzyme responsible for activating vitamin D — we showed that this effect operates through modulation of myostatin and leptin signaling. These findings have direct implications for sarcopenia and obesity, two of the most common and poorly managed conditions in medicine.

[Figure pending extraction]
Mechanism

CYP2R1 and Vitamin D Activation

CYP2R1 is the primary hepatic enzyme that converts vitamin D to its circulating form, 25-hydroxyvitamin D. Our CYP2R1 knockout mice develop sarcopenia, increased adiposity, and disrupted energy sensing — phenotypes that mirror common human diseases. By studying these animals at the molecular level, we are uncovering how vitamin D regulates oxidative phosphorylation, growth signaling, and metabolic homeostasis.

Translation

Immune Modulation and Clinical Targets

Vitamin D's non-calcium effects extend to the immune system. We are investigating CAR-Treg approaches to type 1 diabetes, vitamin D's role in tumor engraftment through GC protein regulation, and its effects on immune development in children. Our clinical translation targets include PCOS, T1DM, and sarcopenia — common diseases where new therapeutic strategies are urgently needed.

Philosophy

How We Do Science

Picking Research Questions

We aim to choose questions that are important — questions with clear clinical relevance for common diseases that are not well managed. We ask these questions using cutting-edge techniques and taking advantage of the unique resources available at UPenn and CHOP. Finally, we target questions where we have a specific advantage, either because of expertise in techniques, collaborators we work with, reagents we possess, or, rarely, knowledge gained from careful parsing of preliminary data.

The Improv Framework

We use principles of improv to help guide how we do science. For instance, we say “Yes, and…” to data that doesn’t fit our preconceived model of how something is supposed to work. The data builds the model.

When we have an interesting finding, we ask “If this is true, what else is true?” in two dimensions. The first dimension is to find an orthogonal way to interrogate the same question: to determine the extent to which the finding is real vs. an artifact of the way we asked the question. The second dimension is to understand what the clinical import or meaning is of the novel finding.

Finally, we aim to “find the game”: that is, to find what is truly meaningful or interesting about a specific result.

The Craftsperson

We strongly recommend you be a craftsperson: someone who takes pride in the quality of their work, makes efforts to improve each day, and figures out how to learn from failures. A craftsperson knows that details are important. A craftsperson measures twice and cuts once. For our complicated experiments, planning well and anticipating problems is the difference between success and failure.

The corollary is that we design our experiments to provide maximal information even in the case of failure or an unexpected outcome. We will fail a lot — but it is the way that we fail that is important. We will fail carefully, with the right controls, so that upon our failure we can build the success of new knowledge.

Farmer, Not Fireperson

We aim to be farmers rather than firepeople. A farmer plans and does a bit of work on several things each day. Some things grow quickly — chickens to eggs to chickens, or for us, cell culture experiments. Other things grow slowly — a knockout mouse is made over six months to a year, bred up over six months, and characterized over another six months to a year.

A productive farmer always has a little work to do (is never bored), always has time to play, and is much more productive than a fireperson — yielding eggs, chickens, corn, wheat, beans, and vegetables. Or for us: a mouse phenotype, a physiology, and a cellular mechanism to explain them both.

In biomedical science the gap between the mediocre and the great is not conception — it is execution. Ideas are cheap. The greatness is in the doing.

We will move the boundaries of human knowledge back a few millimeters.

What It’s Like to Work Here

None of you is in my lab by accident and while I am mistake prone in some areas (see the cuts on my fingers from my time cooking — there is more cut than finger there) I am confident that I have not yet erred in recruiting.

My job as a mentor is fourfold: to help you avoid mistakes I’ve already made, to help you persist after frustrating failures, to shorten the cycle time between tries, and to help you become a better scientist.

If you are a post-doc interested in leaving lab science, or a research associate wanting to go to medical school — telling me that is the best way to get me to help you reach that goal. I want you to be happy and I will try to align the lab’s goals with your goals so that your time here is the most productive for you.
